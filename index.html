Overview

Benchmarking in robotic manipulation is essential for advancing scientific progress, as demonstrated in fields like computer vision and NLP. While prior efforts in robotics have focused on benchmarking various aspects of robotic tasks, the landscape has evolved. We now aim to scale robotics to the level of Large Foundation models, necessitating a shift in how we evaluate robotic tasks and address emerging challenges. Our workshop aims to unite researchers from diverse sub-communities, including robot learning, simulators, foundation models for robotic manipulation, embodied AI, grasping, motion planning, perception, and sim2real, among others. The workshop will focus on three main agendas: The first agenda of the workshop is to highlight the latest existing efforts in benchmarking by featuring talks by researchers actively contributing in the area. The second agenda is to have an abstract/paper submission to hear from the community what they think should be benchmarked in robotics so as to move the field forward. Lastly, the third agenda is to have a panel debate on the following topics:

  • Is benchmarking needed to measure progress in development of robotics foundation models? How else can we compare these models?
  • The role of simulators in benchmarking
  • Standardization (or lack thereof) in datasets, objects and hardware
  • Quantitative protocols that the community can agree on
  • Which robotic tasks can and should be benchmarked?
  • How to benchmark the emerging robotics foundation models?
  • How to benchmark generalist agents?
  • What are the most prominent challenges in benchmarking robotics?
  • How do we incentivize researchers to benchmark their learnt models?
  • Do we even need benchmarking in robot manipulation? When should researchers be disincentivized to benchmark?
This workshop will benefit the robot learning community by potentially replicating the progress seen in the computer vision community with benchmarks like ImageNet, MS COCO, and KITTI. Additionally, constructive feedback on the limitations of current benchmarking efforts will encourage the proposal of innovative benchmarking methods, further promoting robust scientific advancement in the field.
